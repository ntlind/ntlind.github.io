Product Pricing Using Natural Language Processing

written by Nick Lind, Glenn Branscomb, Kate Schofield, Jess Seok for CIS 545

Executive Summary

In this analysis, we examine data from Mercari, an online peer-to-peer shopping app, and develop a statistical model using item condition, shipping terms, brand, product category, and words used in the item title and description. We test three different models (Elastic Net, Ridge Regression, and LightGBM) against a ‘baseline’ model (which didn’t included any NLP features) to measure the effectiveness of NLP features on the basis of out-of-sample error. We find that the Ridge and Light GBM models achieve comparable performance before discussing tradeoffs between accuracy and ease of implementation. Finally, we conclude that both the Ridge and Light GBM perform significantly better than a baseline model without textual information from the item title and description, suggesting that there is tangible business benefit to including these NLP features when Mercari suggests prices to its app customers. We concldue by considering the limitations of our study and mentioning potential opportunity areas for future research.

Section I: Introduction

Problem Domain

Product pricing is one of the most challenging strategic decisions facing today’s business leaders. Pricing strategy determines the amount of value a seller can extract from a given transaction — price too high and the transaction may not take place; price too low and the seller leaves money on the table. This decision is further complicated by the fact that small differences in product attributes or descriptions and marketing efforts can lead to large changes in optimal prices. Further, product pricing has become increasingly complicated at scale. Large ecommerce merchants offer thousands of products across hundreds of different product categories, many of which are manufactured and marketed by third-parties. With scale, it is increasingly important for ecommerce merchants to adopt an automatic, algorithmic approach to predicting optimal prices for each SKU in their inventory.

Online peer-to-peer markets provide an interesting area for further exploration. In these markets, sellers list products and provide identifying characteristics and descriptions. Given the volume of products listed, online platforms need a scalable approach to predicting optimal prices to maximize profits for sellers and, through transaction fees, the platform itself.

Problem Description

Using data from items listed on Mercari, Japan’s largest peer-to-peer shopping app, to develop a statistical model to predict product pricing. Through this analysis, we also explore the following questions:

  • How accurately can we predict a product’s sales price based on its title and description?
  • What words are most influential in determining the sales price? Which words improve sales price and which words decrease it?
  • Does free shipping for the buyer increase the sales price? At what cost is it worth it to offer customers free shipping?
  • Is there a tangible business benefit in using Natural Language Processing to aid pricing decisions?

Insights related to these questions have broad implications for business managers, individual sellers, and ecommerce platforms, and our findings are also likely to generalize well to other peer-to-peer selling platforms (e.g., eBay and Etsy). For example, an understanding of the role of particular words in increasing or decreasing sales price can identify words that sellers should include in the item title or description to increase sales price, and words they should avoid. Investigation of the accuracy of a predictive pricing model that utilizes text from item titles and descriptions can inform business strategy as firms look to implement increasingly sophisticated predictive pricing algorithms.

Section II: Data Preprocessing

Data Overview

The data was obtained from Kaggle and contains information for over 1.4 million product listings on Mercari, Japan’s biggest community-powered shopping app. Founded in 2013, Mercari provides a mobile marketplace for users to easily sell and purchase second-hand items across a wide range of product categories, including fashion, electronics, jewelry, console games, and handmade items. The Mercari app has been downloaded over 40 million times across Japan, the U.K., and the U.S.

The dataset includes the price at which the item was sold (the response variable) as well as six potential explanatory variables which describe various product attributes. These variables are described below.

  • Price: The price that the item was sold for in USD.
  • Name: The title of the listing, cleansed of any text that refers to prices.
  • Item condition: The condition of the item, as reported by the seller. Here, 1 represents “New”, 2 represents “Like New”, 3 represents “Good”, 4 represents “Fair”, and 5 represents “Poor.”
  • Product category: The category of the listing (e.g., “Women/Jewelry/Necklaces” or “Electronics/Computers & Tablets/Components & Parts”).
  • Brand name: The brand of the item.
  • Shipping: Whether or not the shipping fee is paid by the seller.
  • Item description: The full description of the product provided by the seller, cleansed of any text that refers to prices.

Though we believe our dataset to be of very high quality, there are three main limitations with the files that were uploaded to Kaggle. First, the dataset omits information that may be important to predicting the sales price of an item. For example, the data does not include visual information about the quality of the product (e.g., pictures), prices for comparable products, or date at which the sale occurred (which could reduce the validity of our results for seasonal products).

Second, the dataset includes sales prices for transactions that actually occurred. The realized sale price may not be the optimal price at which the item could have sold. The dataset omits information on prior prices (e.g., if the price of an item was reduced over time if it did not sell), prices of similar items that have not yet sold, and the amount of time an item was listed before it was sold. As a result, a predictive pricing algorithm constructed utilizing this dataset must be applied thoughtfully — the predicted prices that it generates may not be optimal given the seller’s preference for a quick sale vs. maximum profits.

Finally, the free-text used to describe each product’s attributes had to be translated from Japanese to English for use in this dataset. These translations were likely completed algorithmically, and there is a high likelihood that at least some of the product attributes were translated incorrectly.

Though the three data limitations above may have a minor effect on our ultimate findings, we do not believe that they will bias our results or prevent us from answering our original research questions.

Data Wrangling Approach

After acquiring our data directly from the Kaggle competition’s home page, our greatest challenge was to efficiently transform our unstructured text into hundreds of thousands of potentially predictive features. After experimenting with multiple transformation pipelines, we were able to successfully handle all 1,482,535 observations and 55,000+ features using extremely sparse matrices via Python’s scipy package. Our final transformation pipeline completed the following tasks:

Data Wrangling Approach

  1. Converted our pandas dataframes to sparse matrices to improve processing times
  2. Transformed our non-NLP features as follows: i) Price: We noticed in our exploratory analysis that our response variable was heavily right-skewed. To reduce the effect of outliers on our response, variable, we transformed this feature such that y=log(price+1)y = log(price + 1)y=log(price+1). ii) Item Condition: We’d hypothesize that there is a non-linear relationship between the different levels of item_condition, which is to say that a one-unit increase in item_condition has a different effect on price when the shift occurs between 1-2 stars and 4-5 stars. To capture this effect, we convert all five levels of this feature into distinct, binary indicators.
  3. Transformed our ‘name’ and ‘item_description’ features using Natural Language Processing (NLP). This entailed: i) Converting each string to lowercase ii) Removing punctuation marks, digits, and common English stopwords to avoid adding noise to our model iii) Tokenizing our strings, effectively splitting them by word iv) Stemming our strings to remove unnecessary suffixes (e.g., ‘beautiful’ would be stemmed to ‘beaut’) v) Lemmatizing our strings to better connect similar, stemmed words (e.g., ‘lay’ and ‘laid’ both become ‘lay’)
  4. Filled missing values with ‘NA’ to preserve the dimensionality of our dataset since a missing name or description could easily serve as an important signal to potential buyers
  5. Created Bag of Words representations (unigrams, bigrams, and trigrams) of our text such that all transformed words were converted to binary indicators
  6. Created term frequency - inverse document frequency (TF-IDF) text representations (unigrams, bigrams, and trigrams) to emphasize words that occur frequently in a given listing while de-emphasising words that occur frequently in many different listings and, in doing so, improve predictive performance
  7. Concatenated our NLP features with item_condition and shipping_fee to create our input dataframe

After merging our NLP features with our transformed price, item_condition, and name features, we split 20% of our data into a validation set and used the remaining 80% to train our models via k-fold cross-validation. Our final dataset dimensions were as follows:

Section III: Implementation and Analysis

After successfully cleaning and transforming our dataset, we implemented our analysis using the following approach:

  • Performed Exploratory Data Analysis to identify hypotheses and validate transformations
  • Built several candidate predictive models using Elastic Net, Ridge regression, and Microsoft’s gradient boosting algorithm (Light GBM). All models were tuned exclusively on our training set using k-fold cross-validation (described below).
  • Evaluated presented models on the basis of out-of-sample error and ease of interpretation and implementation
  • Commented on our findings and consider the risks of our analysis

Model Building

We trained four different models against this extremely sparse training set:

  • Ridge Regression: Chosen to counter the effects of multicollinearity between our NLP features while being both efficient and interpretable.
  • CV Elastic Net: Chosen to explore the trade-off between L1 and L2 regularization and test for potential improvements over our ridge regression.
  • Light Gradient Boosting Machine (Light GBM): Chosen as our boosting algorithm of choice due to its perceived speed advantages over XGBoost and scikit’s GBM.
  • Baseline: To test the impact of our NLP features, we ran an additional Ridge regression that didn’t include any of our generated NLP features

Cross-Validated Elastic Net

We began our modeling exercises with CV Elastic Net since ridge regression is merely the same algorithm with α=0\alpha = 0α=0. We aimed to use Elastic Net’s L1 regularization parameter (λ\lambdaλ) to shrink hundreds of non-essential betas to 0 and, in doing so, reduce the dimensionality of our data. After testing a variety of alpha values ranging from 0.0001 to 0.99, we found our model trying to minimize alpha in an effort to limit dimensionality reduction. This supports our earlier hypothesis that there is a high degree of collinearity between our features, causing Elastic Net to prefer Ridge to LASSO. These findings imply that Ridge regression will outperform Elastic Net and, as a result, we will examine Ridge’s non-zero beta coefficients when exploring the effect of individual tokens against item price (see “Interpretation of Elastic Net Coefficients”, in the next section). In accordance with this hypothesis, we will choose not to perform dimensionality reduction on our dataset as a result of our Elastic Net.

Ridge Regression

After running our 5-fold CV Elastic Net to find the error-minimizing regularization and Elastic Net mixing parameters (λ\lambdaλ = 3), we run Ridge regression to see if we can improve our out-of-sample RMSE using more stringent L2 regularization.

Light GBM

Our final model, Light GBM, is a gradient-boosted tree algorithm currently in development by Microsoft. Commonly used to solve a variety of problems across many different industries, this relatively new gradient-boosted algorithm has repeatedly proven to be more effective than Random Forests when properly tuned. To tune our parameters and avoid overfitting, we used a 10-fold cross-validated model to select the best learning rate and maximum tree depth while minimizing RMSE.

Baseline

Given how much effort it requires to generate and leverage NLP features, we wanted to test whether business managers could really expect to improve their pricing models by including these new features. For this baseline model, we ran a 5-fold cross-validated Ridge regression on our non-NLP features (item_condition, shipping_fee, brand, and category) to better examine the predictive benefit of our NLP efforts.

Our Findings

Final Predictive Model

To evaluate the generalizability of our three models, we used each model to predict product prices for our validation set before calculating the out-of-sample Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Residual Mean Squared Error (RMSE) terms for all three options. As shown by the RMSEs in the summary below, our Light GBM algorithm outperforms our Ridge regression by a narrow margin. When all is said and done, however, we would recommend the ridge regression as our final model due to its relatively parsimonious implementation and expedient run-time. As expected, ridge regression significantly outperformed Elastic Net: because many of our predictors are so highly correlated (e.g., ‘addidas’ as both a brand name and word in the item description), we expect LASSO to have a difficult time in deciding which feature to keep and which to shrink to zero.

According to the Mean Absolute Error terms shown below (measured in dollars), both Ridge regression and Light GBM significantly outperform our baseline model, suggesting that there is tangible business benefit to including NLP in our pricing model.

Real-World Implications

Through this analysis, we’ve shown that business managers can improve their pricing capabilities by using Natural Language Processing to identify and develop predictive features. Though these features can be difficult to harvest and time-consuming to include in the modeling pipeline, Mercari can expect to see a 7.18% improvement in their pricing recommendation engine after implementation. Across Mercari’s 1.4M product listings, this 1.92improvementinpredictingpricingpowercanleadtoa1.92 improvement in predicting pricing power can lead to a1.92improvementinpredictingpricingpowercanleadtoa2,688,000 increase in total value to Mercari’s vendors.

Secondarily, this study emphasizes the importance of offering free shipping to the modern consumer. Any phrases related to packaging or shipping (e.g., “ship stamp”, “envelope”) had a dramatic dampening effect on product price, an effect which may outweigh the cost of offering complementary shipping with each purchase.

Study Limitations and Potential Improvements

A few limitations of this study are as follows:

  • Ideally, we could have tested additional models and features, but we were severely contrained by the computing power of our laptops. Pushing our processing to a cluster in the cloud (e.g., AWS) could help ease this limitation.
  • Given more processing power, we could potentially improve the fit of our models considerably by generating additional NLP features (e.g., n-gram count frequencies for name and item_description).
  • We hypothesize that the picture accompanying the product descriptions is likely to have a major effect on sales price. Given enough computing power, we could potentially deploy methods such as neural net to incorporate additional features describing the quality of each item’s photo.
  • Though we would have liked to produce bootstrapped confidence intervals for each of our final model’s beta coefficients, the processing time was too computationally intensive for our hardware. Given more time, we would prefer to use better machinery or a more efficient programming language to create such intervals.
  • Lastly, we could consider using clustering techniques that have been proven to work with sparse datasets (e.g., Restricted Boltzmann machines) to create additional training features.
