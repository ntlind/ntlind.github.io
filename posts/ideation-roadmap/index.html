The Ideation Roadmap

Introduction

Building great products requires much more than just a simple checklist, but sometimes all we need is an idea and a place to start. The guide below was stitched-together from a number of impressive reads that my mentors and friends have recommended to me over the years:

  • This is Marketing (Seth Godin): This book is a must-read. Excellent, succinct guide which heavily influenced the “Discovery” section of these notes.
  • Product Marketing Debunked (Yasmeen Tur): Provides a great framework for thinking about PMM from Discovery to all the way to go-to-market.
  • The First 100 Customers (Ben Lee): A thorough, rambling collection of notes with several hidden gems.
  • Talking to Humans (Frank Rimalovski and Giff Constable): Unsurprisingly, the central theme of this book is “get out there and connect with people”. Provides an overview of where you might look and what you might ask if you wanted to gather information from potential customers.
  • Dynamic Marketing Strategy (MKTG 612 at Wharton): This classes gave a high-level overview of many of these subjects, but didn’t go deep enough to really be actionable.
  • startup idea checklist (Slava Akhmechet): A quick, bulleted framework with excellent content.
  • Sam Altman’s Startup Playbook: Sam’s guide to YC’s idea development philosophy. Sam offers a number of interesting startup insights that fall beyond the scope of ideation.
  • The Artist’s Way: This book is more about the creative process than about entrepreneurship or product development, but there’s plenty of overlap that went into this guide.

Phase 1: Discovery

Your goal: Learn as much as you can about yourself, your customers, and your product concept.

Answer meta questions to clarify your intent

  • Why are you really here? Why go through all of this trouble?

  • What is the higher calling of this idea? What’s your mission?

    • Start with an action verb
    • Make it short and easy to remember
    • Focus on a very specific problem
    • Have a big goal in mind
    • Write in plain language
    • Avoid including the word ‘help’ - be bold and confident
  • What about this idea excites you?
  • What about this idea scares you?
  • What values will your company stand for?
  • On a scale of 1-10, how passionate are you to solve this problem?
  • How much time and capital are you willing to devote to this effort?
  • What are you willing to give up to make this idea work?

  • What community of people will you begin to lead if you create this product?

    Conduct customer research

  • Conduct customer research with a cross-functional team
  • Frame a few initial hypotheses, keeping them as general as possible

  • Test hypothesis with a large audience (as many people as possible). Questions to ask may include:

    • Would you purchase this product? If so, for how much?
    • On a scale of 1-5, how interested are you in solving this problem?

  • Run detailed interview studies for 5-7 participants per segment once you have a specific product idea in mind. Questions to ask include:

    • B2C

      • How do you make purchasing decisions?
      • What are your personal / business goals for the year?
      • Tell me about your interest in [insert market]
      • How long have you been interested in this market?
      • What are some of your motivations for being a part of this industry and exploring this ecosystem?
      • What’s your philosophy around this market?
      • What type of pain points or problems do you experience in this [market or activity]?
      • How do you find solution to those pain points?
      • How big are these pain points on a scale of 1-10?
      • How would you describe or portray yourself in the context of this industry?
      • Tell me about how you looked for a product or solution to solve your problem?
      • When did you purchase this product?
      • Where were you at the time of the purchase?
      • What are some of the alternatives that you looked into before you purchased our product?
      • Did anyone else influence your decision? If so, who?
      • How did you hear about this product?
      • When did you first want to buy this product?
      • How long did it take you to decide to purchase the product?
      • What kind of solutions did you try? Or not try? Why not?
      • Did you ask anyone else about what they thought about the purchase?
      • Did you have any anxiety or hesitation about the purchase?
      • How often do you purchase the product?
      • How is your problem now solved?

    • B2B

      • What are your short term and long-term goals for the year?
      • How do you think about your revenue?
      • How big is your company (yearly revenue)?
      • What is your primary driver of revenue?
      • How are you planning on meeting your revenue targets this year?
      • What alternative products do you currently use?
      • How many customers do you have and what’s that growth rate look like?
      • What are some of the adjacent or third-party tools you use today?
      • How would you use this product?
      • What is your focus on business objective for the year ahead?
      • What are your product goals for the year ahead?
      • Are you using a competitor today across any of our product?
      • Are you using any demand generation or lead generation tools?
      • How large are your product and development teams?
      • How big is the pain point of the problem you’re trying to solve on a scale of 1-10?
      • What’s the alternative to what people are using today?

    • Both

      • What should I have asked you that I didn’t?

  • Synthesize segments and key themes, being sure to calculate metrics to avoid biases

    • Write-down key themes from customer interviews, including:

      • Topics that keep coming up
      • Jobs that need doing

    • Identify all possible consumer segments, making sure that each segment is:

      1. Identifiable: Can you measure characteristics, usage, and behavior?
      2. Substantial: Is each segment you’ve identified large enough to be profitable?
      3. Accessible: Can you reach each segment via both communication and distribution channels?
      4. Stable: Are the behaviors, hangouts, and lifestyles of your segments going to hold, or do they fluctuate?
      5. Differentiable: How clear are the needs of each segment? What makes each segment unique?
      6. Actionable: Are you positive that each segment truly needs your product? Who needs it more than others?

    • Select which segments you want to target and capture:

      • Identifiers of the group you’re targeting, focusing on the where, why and how vs. who, what, and when
      • Jobs that need to be done (pain points)
      • Emotions they want to feel
      • Estimated % of customer universe
      • Key worries / frustrations
      • Key loves / delights
      • KPIs and related obstacles
      • Misc. Patterns
      • Age
      • Sex
      • Income
      • Education
      • Occupation
      • Relationship Status
      • Language
      • Ethnicity
      • Interests
      • Social media platforms
      • News & media sources
      • Favorite hangouts
      • Where you can find them for a conversation
      • How they communicate
      • What blogs they read and why?
      • How they spend their free time
      • What other products they’re buying and why
      • Stage of their life
    • Select the segments which you aren’t going to go after; which segments aren’t for you?

Estimate size-of-the-prize

  • Total market size (e.g., US, global)
  • Willingness-to-pay (annual)
  • Potential annual revenue
  • Penetration rate of adoption

  • Product of the above = estimated size-of-prize

    Research external forces

    • Suppliers
    • Substitutes
    • Competitors
    • Channels
    • Complementors
    • Outside risks
    • Regulations / Tariffs
    • Cultural / demographic shifts
    • Industry trends
    • Piracy / counterfeiting
    • Insurance
    • Safety

Phase 2: Product Formulation

Your goal: Now that you know who you’re serving, conceive of a product to address their need.

Define your product’s purpose:

  • Why does your product exist?

  • What is your product’s job to be done?

    • My product is for people who believe ___. I will focus on people who want ___. I promise that engaging with what I make will help you get ___.
  • How will the product work?
  • What makes your product better than anything else in the world?
  • What is extreme / remarkable about this idea?
  • What challenges will we face when building this idea?

Narrowly define your customer base:

  • Which segment is desperately looking for this product? Who will be the customer who loves your product enough to not only give it a chance, but also to share it with their friends?
  • What is the essence of their dissatisfaction? If they read this answer, would they say “thanks, I wish I’d thought of putting it that way”?
  • Who needs this product but isn’t looking?
  • Who doesn’t need this product? Who is this product not for?
  • Write a tweet from a hypothetical customer explaining the product and how it eliminates their dissatisfaction.
  • What “metrics of goodness” do your target customers care about? Does your product dominate every available alternative on these metrics?

  • What do we want our customers to feel?

    • Adventure
    • Affection
    • Stability
    • Belonging
    • Connection
    • Control
    • Creativity
    • Community
    • Delight
    • Freedom of expression
    • Freedom of movement
    • Friendship
    • Good looks
    • Good health
    • Learning new things
    • Luxury
    • Nostalgia
    • Obedience
    • Participation
    • Peace of mind
    • Physical activity
    • Power
    • Reassurance
    • Reliability
    • Respect
    • Revenge
    • Romance
    • Sex
    • Safety
    • Security
    • Status
    • Strength
    • Sympathy
    • Tension
  • During which customer touchpoints / channels will each segment interact with this product?
  • How will customers learn about this product?
  • How much education will my target customers need about this product?

Frame your customer message in five key statements:

  • Problem statement: the problem your product solves
  • Positioning statement: what your product does to solve the problem
  • Value proposition: why customers need your product
  • Mission statement: what values does your company stand for?

  • Tagline: 3-6 words that articulate your company / product in a deliberate way

    • Pro tip: marketing slogans are a promise (e.g., ‘Roll tide’ <- promise of dominance)
  • Template: My idea (working product name) is for (target customer) who have (list specific problem). My product solves this issue by (explain solution) because I believe (list values).

Phase 3: Product Roadmap & Go-to-Market Plan

Your goal: Formulate 10 key hypotheses and develop a product roadmap and go-to-market plan that will enable you to test those hypotheses.

Formulate the 10 hypotheses that need to be true for your business to be successful

  • Which are most risky? List them in riskiest order
  • How will you validate these hypotheses in order of risk? Who will you need to speak to?

Prepare product roadmap

  • What are our key product differentiators / features? How should we prioritize development?
  • What’s the smallest market we can survive on? Which of our market sizing assumptions are most at risk?
  • Once we get our first 1000 true fans, how might we expand into the broader market?
  • How are you going to do this? Write it down and put a date on it.
  • Who are you going to do this with?
  • What are our current resource constraints?
  • In a perfect world, what other features or capabilities would your product eventually need?

  • Ask propelling questions (questions where you introduce a constraint)

    • E.g., How would we build a durable table for only $5?
  • What do we have in abundance? What could we trade to others for needed resources
  • What emotions should your customers have when they interact with your product
  • What features should be developed in future MVPs?

Develop go-to-market plan

  • The What

    • What does this product comprise of?
    • What problem does it solve?
    • What are the feature sets?
    • What one thing will most impact the user experience?
    • What is the value prop?
    • What does success look like in alpha, beta, and GA?
    • What exists today?
    • What is main alternative to this product?
    • What price should we charge?

  • The Who

    • Who is the target customer
    • Which communication channels should we leverage to access our target customers?
    • Which channels should we sell through?

  • The Why

    • Why are we building this product?
    • Does this product answer the needs of the market?
    • Why does it make sense to build the product now?
    • Why does this product matter to users?

  • The When

    • When should we launch and become GA (generally available)?
    • When should we start testing beta, early access, and pilot?
    • When should we align our commitments with key stakeholders?
    • When do we need to have legal agreements signed with partners?
    • When do internal teams need to be trained?
    • When will we operationally be ready to launch from a cross-functional perspective?
    • When should we start reaching our target customers?

  • The Where

    • Where should we host our beta?
    • Where should the product launch occur first (local, domestic, international)?
    • Where do partners offer their solutions today (limits, restrictions)?
    • Where can partners access the products?

  • The How

    • How does this product work for user’s today?
    • How will it functionally work and interact with users (apps, hardware, software, data)?
    • How does it help customers today?
    • How many times will customers use the product (days per week, etc.)?
    • Do users need data points or historical information to make sense of the product?
    • How will internal teams need to interact together?
    • Do we have to educate the customer?
    • Do we need a third-party partner?

Phase 4: Build

Your goal: Know exactly what you’re going to build over the next dozen sprints and what you’re going to leave for the future.

Build first MVP

  • Criteria

    • It doesn’t have to function
    • It doesn’t have to scale
    • There are endless ways to create it
    • “If you aren’t embarrassed by your first product, then you released too late”

  • Questions your MVP should answer:

    • Should this product even be built?
    • Does a market exist for my product to thrive in?
    • Which customer opinions really matter?
    • Which core features take priority?
    • What’s essential?
    • Does this product really deliver value to my customers?
    • What’s the right price point for this product?

Conduct validation interviews

  • Who to connect with

    • Friends in your network
    • People in your original research set
    • Influencers
    • People whose opinions you respect from different fields
    • Anyone on social media (email templates on page 66 of “First 100..“)
    • Choose people with at least one degree of separation away from you
    • Be creative (and don’t expect people to come to you)
    • Fish where the fish are (and not where they aren’t)
    • Build-in entry survey for current product
    • Ask people you interview for referrals
    • People who are bored in line
    • Send out an online survey and follow-up with in-person calls

Synthesize validation results and summarize key themes from interviews

  • Are we targeting the right customer segment?
  • Does our product solve a real need for our segment?
  • Are they willing to pay us to solve this problem?
  • Were any of our 10 hypotheses disproven in such a way that we might rethink our idea?
  • Why are the people who don’t like your product right?

Phase 5: Iterate over-and-over until you’ve built something that your customers are proud to use

“The last step is so often overlooked: The part where you show up, regularly, consistently and generously, for years and years, to organize and lead and build confidence in the change you seek to make.”
- Seth Godin
