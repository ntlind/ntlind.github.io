Machine learning's silent killer

Picture this: we've just come up with an ingenious use case for machine learning at our company or client. When we bring the idea to our team, they're immediately intrigued: not only would our idea be relatively straightforward to develop, but it comes with a clear business case and the opportunity to solve a real pain point in ours users' lives. Our executive stakeholders are just as enthused and immediately grant us the funding we need to develop a proof-of-concept. Before we know it, we're getting pings and emails from managers and data scientists on other teams who heard about what we're whipping up and are wondering how they can get involved. "It's clear we've got a winner on our hands," you hear someone say, "if only we can find the data".

Unfortunately, this is where the rose-colored glasses come off: getting access to clean, reliable data is always more difficult than we might expect. There's a reason that data exploration and transformation takes up more than 70% of our time on any given pilot: data issues are unique to the problem at hand and require significant investigation and cross-functional collaboration to resolve.

First, we have to find where the data lives and who can provide us access. This step is usually harder then it sounds: large enterprises will have well-structured data sitting in a dozen different silos (each with their own owner), while startups and smaller companies lack the time and funding to invest in convenient database infrastructure. If we're lucky, we'll be able to work with daily users to map the systems we need to pull from and gain access within a few days.

 

The real work begins after we're able to pull everything into our development environment. Iterative data exploration and issue resolution is the most important step in the data science workflow, and we'll spend the next few hours ensuring that our data is representative of reality. This step (often called data cleaning or data wrangling) isn't nearly as glamorous as incorporating predictive models into our ML pipeline, but skimping at this stage is a recipe for disaster. Amazon's recent foray into automated recruiting serves as a cautionary tale: if we don't think critically about the hidden biases in our data, we could inadvertently do more harm than good.

 

At the surface, many of the issues we're looking for in the data wrangling process are easy to spot and rectify:

 

  • Erroneous values or mislabeled entries for categorical variables
  • Unexpected summary statistics (min, median, mode, etc.)
  • Missing values
  • Unusual outliers
  • Duplicate values

 

The presence of one or more of these little issues, however, can signal a deeper problem that may not have an easy solution:

  • *Sampling biases*: Our training data excludes some particular subgroup or generally isn't representative of the overall population we'll pull from in the future, making our models useless in the real world.
  • *Off-by-one errors*: One of our upstream loops, joins, or maps didn't behave how we expected and, as a result, one of our variables no longer mean what we think they mean.
  • *Stationarity*: The distribution of a particular feature changes over time, limiting the usefulness of historic training data.
  • *Heteroscedasticity*: The variance of one of our features changes as our target variable changes, indicating measurement error or subpopulation differences within our dataset.
  • *Skewed variables*: Our variables exhibit a strong left or right-leaning skew which could cause our models to overfit on outliers if we don't transform (i.e., standardize, normalize) our data prior to training.
  • *Incomplete joins*: We tried to join two different datasets together but, due to differences in the two key columns that we used, the join didn't work as expected
  • *Improper encodings*: The way we're treating our variables is causing our models to make assumptions that aren't indicative of reality (e.g., encoding product categories using a simple index; using a linear scale for months of the year).
  •  
  • Unique time series problems (heteroscedasticity, stationarity)

 

To spot these issues, we'll need to oscillate between generating hypotheses about where our data comes from, testing those hypotheses using visualizations and statistics, and validating any assumptions that arise with our data owner. This process is both time-consuming and thought-demanding, but it's meant to be more of an iterative process throughout our build: we'll find some issues up front, but many will remain uncovered until after we've started interpreting our models. What matters most is that we stay in "exploration mode" for as long as possible and build-in plenty of time to fix any issues that arise.
